Contents of a cigarette

We all know there are many harmful chemicals inside a cigarette. Here are the most commonly found ones.

Nicotine

Nicotine is a colourless, poisonous alkaloid derived from the tobacco plant. It is a powerful drug, which affects the brain and quickly becomes addictive.

Tar

Tar is a sticky brown substance that forms when tobacco cools and condenses. It collects in the lungs and can cause cancer.

Carbon monoxide

An odourless, colourless gas that is released from burning tobacco. When it is inhaled it enters the blood stream and interferes with the working of the heart and the blood vessels.

Arsenic

Arsenic-containing pesticides used in tobacco farming occur in small quantities in cigarette smoke. Arsenic is commonly found in rat poison.

Ammonia

Ammonia is a toxic, colourless gas with a sharp odour. Ammonia compounds are commonly used in cleaning products and fertilisers.

Acetone

Acetone is a colourless, highly volatile and flammable liquid with a characteristic pungent odour. Acetone is used to dissolve many plastics and some synthetic fibers.

3 possible illnesses from smoking

Lung Cancer

Smoking cigarettes increase your risk of developing lung cancer and other types of cancer, such as oesophageal cancer and mouth cancer . Lung cancer is one of the most common and serious types of cancer. Symptoms include persistent cough, coughing up blood and persistent breathlessness.

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)

COPD refers to a group of diseases that cause airflow blockage and breathing-related problems. It includes emphysema and chronic bronchitis . Symptoms include incresing breathlessness, persistent cough with phlegm and frequent chest infections. The harmful chemicals in cigarette smoke can damage the lining of the lungs and airways. Some research also suggests that being exposed to other people's cigarette smoke may increase your risk of COPD.

Coronary Heart Disease (CHD)

Coronary heart disease is the term that describes what happens when your heart's blood supply is blocked or interrupted by a build-up of residue in the coronary arteries from cigarette smoke. Symptoms include chest pain, shortness of breath and feeling faint. Both nicotine and carbon monoxide put a strain on the heart by making it work faster. They also increase your risk of blood clots. Other chemicals in cigarette smoke can damage the lining of your coronary arteries, leading to furring of the arteries.
